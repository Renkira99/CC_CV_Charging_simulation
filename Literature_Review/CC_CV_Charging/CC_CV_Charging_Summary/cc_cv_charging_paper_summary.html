⚡ TL;DR — What All 8 Papers Are About

  • CC-CV charging = Constant Current first (charges battery fast), then Constant Voltage (tops it off safely). This is THE standard for Li-ion batteries in EVs.
  • The big problem: Traditional LLC converters need wide frequency changes to do CC-CV, which kills efficiency and makes ZVS (soft switching) hard.
  • The common solution: Use resonant converters (LCL, LCC, LC) that have inherent CC or CV characteristics at their resonant frequency — no complex control needed!
  • 3 papers focus on wireless charging (WPT) — transmitting power through air gaps (100-200mm) using magnetic resonance coupling.
  • 1 paper tackles thermal management — a multi-stage CC-CV method that monitors battery temperature to charge 20% faster.
  • Key achievement across papers: Efficiencies from 82% (wireless) to 98.8% (wired resonant) with ZVS/ZCS soft switching.

1
A Novel LCL Resonant Converter With Inherent CC-CV Output for On-Board Chargers of Plug-In Electric Vehicles
2023 IEEE Trans. Power Electronics Wired · On-Board η 96.7%
▼
🔴 Problem

Conventional LLC converters use pulse-frequency-modulation (PFM) to achieve CC-CV charging, but this requires a wide switching frequency range, causing poor efficiency and loss of zero-voltage-switching (ZVS) in CC mode.

🟢 Solution

A novel LCL resonant converter that operates at a fixed resonant frequency with inherent CC and CV output characteristics. As battery voltage increases during charging, the tank naturally transitions: LCL tank → CC mode, LC tank → CV mode.

🔵 How It Works
  • Uses a reconfiguration rectifier circuit with a clamping branch
  • At low battery voltage → LCL tank provides constant current output
  • At high battery voltage → equivalent LC tank provides constant voltage output
  • No sensing or control circuits needed for CC-CV transition — it's inherent!
  • Wide ZVS for primary switches and ZCS for secondary diodes maintained
🟢 Key Results
Power: 1 kW
Input: 400 V
Output: 250–400 V
Full-load η: 96.7%
CC mode η: >96%
💡 Key Takeaway

Fixed-frequency operation + inherent CC-CV = simpler, cheaper, more reliable on-board charger. No need for complex feedback control for mode switching.

2
An Inherent CC-CV Charging Architecture for Dual Receiver Semi-Dynamic Resonant Inductive Wireless EV Charging System
2024 IEEE Trans. Transportation Electrification Wireless · Semi-Dynamic
▼
🔴 Problem

Semi-dynamic wireless charging (charging while vehicle moves slowly or is parked) suffers from power null phenomenon (PNP) — dead spots where no power transfer happens — plus power fluctuations and poor efficiency.

🟢 Solution
  • Dual receiver (Rx) coupling architecture eliminates PNP by ensuring continuous magnetic coupling
  • Inherent CC-CV hybrid compensation provides automatic charging profile without DC-DC chopper
  • PID feedback controller for optimal power delivery across multiple transmitter (Tx) coils
  • Unipolar square pads with optimal spacing to maximize cross-coupling inductance
🔵 How It Works

Multiple transmitter coils are embedded in the road/parking surface. Two receivers on the vehicle ensure that at least one is always coupled to a transmitter. The resonance magnetic field coupling (RMFC) architecture ensures coupling effectiveness along the entire charging lane. CC-CV output is achieved automatically even under misalignment conditions.

💡 Key Takeaway

Eliminates the need for complex DC-DC converters and control strategies. The dual-Rx architecture is key to solving the dead-spot problem in dynamic/semi-dynamic wireless charging.

3
CC–CV Wireless EV Charging With Power Balance Control in Primary- and Secondary-Side Converters
2025 IEEE Access Wireless · WPT η 82% (SS)
▼
🔴 Problem

Wireless EV charging systems using conventional PI controllers have slow settling times (400-600ms) and struggle with stable CC-CV operation across varying load conditions.

🟢 Solution

Power Balance Control (PBC) applied to both primary-side (PS) and secondary-side (SS) converters. Tested with a 72V, 50Ah Li-ion NMC battery.

🟢 Key Results
SS peak η: 82%
PS peak η: 77%
PS settling: 600→100ms
SS settling: 400→20ms
Battery: 72V 50Ah NMC
💡 Key Takeaway

Secondary-side control wins. SS converter adapts its input resistance to match the optimal load of the IPT coils, giving better efficiency AND faster response than primary-side control. Highest losses occur in CC mode (in coils and HF inverter).

4
Fixed-Frequency LCC Resonant Battery Charger With Inherent CC-CV Operation for Electric Vehicles
2025 IEEE Trans. Industrial Electronics Wired · Isolated η 98.3%
▼
🔴 Problem

Existing resonant chargers need mode-change switches or extra components to transition between CC and CV modes, adding complexity and cost.

🟢 Solution

An isolated half-bridge LCC resonant charger that simultaneously uses the CC characteristic of LCC converters and CV characteristic of LC converters at resonant frequency — no mode-change switch needed.

🔵 How It Works
  • LCC resonant tank inherently outputs constant current
  • LC resonant tank inherently outputs constant voltage
  • Combined topology transitions naturally as battery voltage rises
  • ZVS at turn-ON for all inverter switches, ZCS at turn-OFF for all rectifier diodes
  • Conventional PWM control for output voltage/current regulation
🟢 Key Results
Power: 100 W
Input: 340 V
Output V: 50–90 V
Output I: 0.2–2 A
Max η: 98.3%
💡 Key Takeaway

Intrinsic CC-CV with zero extra components for mode switching. Simplest structure among similar approaches with near-99% efficiency.

2023 IEEE INOCON Conference Wired · On-Board
▼
🔴 Problem

Standard LLC converters with variable frequency control have a wide switching frequency range and also lose ZVS during CC charging mode.

🟢 Solution

An LCL resonant converter exploiting the inherent current-source and voltage-source characteristics of the LCL topology. Includes a design technique to achieve desired output voltage range in both CC and CV modes.

🟢 Key Results
Power: 3.6 kW
Input: 250 V
Output: 126.3–168 V
ZVS: ✓ Full range
💡 Key Takeaway

Provides a design methodology for LCL converters with natural CC-CV. Eliminates sensing/control circuits while maintaining ZVS across the entire charging range. Higher power (3.6kW) compared to other prototypes.

6
Load Independent Constant Current and Constant Voltage Control of LCC-Series Compensated Wireless EV Charger
2022 IEEE Trans. Power Electronics Wireless · WPT η 93.8%
▼
🔴 Problem

Wireless EV chargers struggle with load-dependent performance — as the battery charges, its impedance changes, affecting current/voltage regulation and efficiency.

🟢 Solution

LCC-Series compensation topology designed for load-independent operation. CC charging uses zero phase angle and soft switching. CV charging uses a semibridgeless active rectifier with pulse density modulation (PDM) control — no wireless communication needed!

🟢 Key Results
Output V: 210 V
Output I: 5 A
Power: 1 kW
Air gap: 150 mm
Max η: 93.8%
💡 Key Takeaway

No wireless communication needed between primary and secondary sides. Load-independent operation means the charger performs consistently regardless of battery state. Best wireless efficiency in this collection at 93.8%.

7
Simple-Structure Fixed-Frequency Isolated Resonant Converter for EV Battery Charger Applications With CC–CV Charge Method
2024 IEEE Trans. Transportation Electrification Wired · Isolated η 98.8%
▼
🔴 Problem

CC-CV converters have output current variation in CC mode, and efficiency drops during CV mode due to lack of synchronous rectification.

🟢 Solution

A reconfigurable resonant converter (RC) with a dual resonant network. An auxiliary switch serves two purposes: blocks the feed-forward path in CC mode (minimizing current variation) and acts as a synchronous rectifier in CV mode (boosting efficiency).

🔵 How It Works
  • CC mode: LCC converter inherent CC characteristic at resonant frequency
  • CV mode: Series LC converter inherent CV characteristic at resonant frequency
  • Auxiliary switch reconfigures the dual resonant network for mode transition
  • Fixed frequency operation throughout
  • ZVS for switches, ZCS for diodes
🟢 Key Results
Power: 180 W
Input: 340 V
Output V: 50–90 V
Output I: 0.2–2 A
Max η: 98.8% 🏆
💡 Key Takeaway

Highest efficiency in the entire collection at 98.8%. The dual-purpose auxiliary switch is clever — it fixes CC-mode current variation AND boosts CV-mode efficiency with synchronous rectification.

8
Multi-Stage Constant Current–Constant Voltage Under Constant Temperature (MSCC-CV-CT) Charging Technique for Li-ion Batteries in Light Weight EVs
2023 Springer · Electrical Engineering Thermal Management 20% Faster Charging
▼
🔴 Problem

Standard CC-CV charging doesn't consider battery temperature, leading to thermal degradation, reduced battery life, and slower charging. Fast charging generates excessive heat.

🟢 Solution

A Multi-Stage CC-CV under Constant Temperature (MSCC-CV-CT) method that uses cell temperature as the primary control metric. Combined with pulse current charging and a series resonant converter to further reduce temperature rise.

🔵 How It Works
  • Closed-loop controller monitors battery SoC, temperature, and internal resistance
  • Charging current is dynamically adapted based on thermal conditions
  • Multi-stage current scheme: starts high, reduces as temperature rises
  • Pulse current charging + series resonant converter further limit heat
  • Can increase initial current above 2C for even faster charging
🟢 Key Results
Charge time: 55.8 min (vs 85 min CC-CV)
Temp rise: 6.5°C (vs 7.5°C)
Speed: 20% faster
Temp reduction: 20% at same time
💡 Key Takeaway

The only paper in this collection focused on battery health. Temperature-aware charging is crucial for real-world EV applications. The 20% faster charging while keeping temperature lower is a significant practical improvement.

🧠 Reference

Key Concepts Cheat Sheet

CC-CV

CC-CV Charging

Constant Current charges the battery at a fixed current until the voltage limit is reached. Then Constant Voltage holds voltage steady while current tapers to a cut-off value. This prevents overcharging and extends battery life.

Topology

LLC Resonant Converter

Inductor-Inductor-Capacitor tank. High efficiency, widely used in OBCs. Problem: needs wide frequency range for CC-CV.

Topology

LCL Resonant Converter

Inductor-Capacitor-Inductor tank. Inherently acts as a current source — perfect for CC mode without complex control.

Topology

LCC Resonant Converter

Inductor-Capacitor-Capacitor tank. Inherent CC characteristic. When combined with LC (CV), gives automatic CC-CV transition.

Switching

ZVS / ZCS

Zero Voltage Switching & Zero Current Switching — soft switching techniques that slash switching losses by switching when V or I is zero. Critical for high efficiency.

WPT

Wireless Power Transfer

Uses magnetic resonance coupling across an air gap (50-200mm). Compensation networks (LCC-S, SS, etc.) tune the system for efficient power transfer.

WPT

Power Null Phenomenon

Dead spots in dynamic wireless charging where the vehicle receiver passes between transmitter coils and receives zero power. Solved with dual receivers or overlapping coils.

Battery

Li-Ion Battery

Dominant EV battery chemistry. High energy density, long life. Requires CC-CV charging to prevent overcharging (which causes thermal runaway and degradation).

Battery

State of Charge (SoC)

The battery's "fuel gauge" — percentage of remaining capacity. CC mode charges from ~10-80% SoC fast, CV mode handles the last 80-100% carefully.

Thermal

Temperature Management

Battery temperature rises during fast charging. MSCC-CV-CT method dynamically adjusts charging current based on temperature to prevent degradation.

Control

PWM vs PFM

PWM = fixed frequency, variable duty cycle. PFM = variable frequency. Fixed-frequency (PWM) converters are preferred because they simplify EMI filter design.

Control

Pulse Density Modulation

Controls power by varying the number of active switching pulses per cycle. Used in Paper 6 for load-independent CV regulation in wireless charging — no wireless communication needed.
